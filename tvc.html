Thrust Vector Control Rocket

Introduction

While browsing YouTube one day, I came across a channel called BPS.Space.

At the time (the channel does many rocket-things now), it was about building model rockets which could stabalize themselves using Thrust Vector Control (TVC).

Thrust Vectoring is when a vehicle (like a rocket or plane) manipulates the direction of its thrust to change its direction and angular velocity.

The channel inspired me to build my own TVC rocket. This page is a documentation of my progress, as well as why and how I do things on this project. I will update it periodically.

Engine Mount Design

(February 4th, 2023)

As of now, I nearly have a fully finished (prototype) version of an engine mount. The servos are not yet connected, and part of the arm is broken. I hope to finish and test it soon.

Every 3D printed part of this rocket has so far been designed in Tinkercad. Though maybe not the most professional option, it does everything I need it to do efficiently, conviently, and for free. All the parts are printed on my Creality Ender 3 Pro:

The first verion of the main ring looked like this:

At the time, I had not used much CAD, so it was very rough. After printing it I quickly realised I couldn't even screw in the servo screws. After few variations of just the ring, I "got distracted" by other projects. But through those, like from this fully automatic rubber band gun with removable magazines:

I gained a lot of design experience. After finding a datasheet with the servo dimensions, I was able to design this version fairly quick and without much trouble, despite the unsual round space constraints of the rocket body:

The small darker brown ring in the center will hold the 29mm motor (the tan cylinder). The servo on the arm will be connected with a rod to this. The motor can either be glued into the ring, friction fitted by increasing its radius with electrical tape, compressed by further tightening the screws holding the ring, or a combination of these methods. The ring is small so it can simply be thrown away (if the motor is glued in) and reprinted quickly.

The plastic parts will be connected to the servos using stiff bent metal wire I have laying around.

Unfortunatley, my printer was not cooperating, and it would take many attempts to print everything. Eventally (after much trouble shooting, changing the bowden tube, and fixing other under extrusion issues) I had everything printed, except for the green arm. Not only would this part fail multiple times half way through, but I kept breaking it while taking off supports.

After some trial and error, I eventually got an intact one, but ignorantly overtightend one of the screws, and tore through the plastic.

This is where I'm at with the mount at the moment (the overtightend arm is actually the one used in the pictures of it assembled, beucase it still works, just not ideally). The next step would be to reprint the arm, put the two rings and the arm together, and test its motion with a lit engine and my flight computer at the helm.
